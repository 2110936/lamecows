A Lame Problem to Have

Tackling Lameness in Cattle

Posted on 11/11/2021
by 2110936

As the owner of an extremely exuberant puppy, I am welcomed home from work every evening with a great deal of affection; he bounds towards me, tail wagging back and forth energetically, and smothers me in hundreds of slobbery kisses. So, when I walked through the door last weekend and found that his usual bound had been replaced by a seemingly painful limp, I was immediately concerned and took him straight to the vet’s office. Somehow, in all of his outdoor antics, a thorn had become firmly lodged in his paw. With the thorn removed (and a lot of sympathy treats!), he was back to running laps around the living room a few days later.

The prevalence of lameness

Cattle, on the other hand, are often not so lucky. Whilst I was able to identify and resolve my pup’s lameness with relative ease, the challenge is much greater for farmers who are faced with large herds of cattle, and this is particularly an issue for the dairy industry1. Indeed, a recent investigation in the UK comprising 29,760 cows revealed that the average prevalence of lameness was 17%2, which is only a marginal decline from the 20.6% figure identified in 19903. Arguably, it will never be possible to eradicate lameness in herds entirely due to its complex aetiology1. We can, however, continue to tackle the lameness endemic by training farmers to promptly identify and respond to lameness in their herds, as well as promoting methods of prevention.

What causes lameness?

Perhaps the best place to start is with the recognition that approximately 90% of all lameness cases stem from diseases of the foot4. Approximately two thirds of these cases are thought to arise from non-infectious diseases5. For instance, in a similar way to my puppy, cows may become lame if they encounter a foreign body which subsequently causes disruption of the horn of the claws. Other non-infectious culprits include ulcers, sole haemorrhages, abscesses, vertical fissures and heel erosion4. The remaining cases can be attributed to infectious diseases; interdigital phlegmon, colloquially referred to as ‘foot rot’, has been regarded as the most important lesion of the foot6, followed closely by bovine digital dermatitis7.

Ultimately, the housing and management factors on each farm contribute to a herd’s overall susceptibility to these diseases8. For instance, cows who are forced to stand on bare, concrete floors for prolonged periods of time will sustain greater hoof damage than those who are provided with adequate bedding5. Steep steps and sloped surfaces are also known to exacerbate this risk. So, to mitigate the risk of lameness, farmers need to be thoroughly evaluating their management practices. Do the cows have clean, comfortable surfaces on which they can lie, or are they forced to stand on concrete and manure? What is the quality of the pathway between the pasture and the milking parlour? By considering these factors and amending their practices, farmers should be able to reduce the prevalence of lameness on their farms to an extent.

Assessing lameness

However, even if all of the best practices are followed, the need remains for farmers to be able to identify and remedy lameness in their cattle. This is especially true in the dairy industry, where lameness is considered to be the most crucial welfare problem for the dairy cow9, leading to decreased milk production and, more importantly, severe pain5. Given that lameness is regarded as an inability to walk optimally, it follows that any cow displaying clear reluctance to bear weight on one or more of their hooves can be assumed to be lame8. However, indicators of lameness are often much less clear-cut than this; instead, farmers may need to utilise a gait scoring system10. This involves assessing each cow’s overall ability to walk and assigning them a numerical score, typically ranging from one (sound) to five (severely lame). In this assessment, observers should be alert to indicators such as back arching, short strides, jerky head bopping and joint stiffness8.

Treatment of lameness

If a farmer discovers that a member of the herd is lame, it is crucial that they intervene as soon as possible; lameness does not resolve itself, and any delay in intervention will further compromise the animal’s welfare11. Depending on the cause of lameness, treatment may comprise corrective trimming, foot-bathing or the use of antibiotics12—this list is not exhaustive! Ultimately, treatment should be performed in consultation with a vet to ensure the best outcome for the herd.

If farmers were to respond to lameness in cattle with the same speed in which I scooped up my puppy and rushed him off to the vet, perhaps it would be possible to put an end to the lameness endemic. We still have a long way to go, but if we continue to promote methods of prevention and the importance of early intervention, we might just make the lives of cattle better.
